What is Degrowth?

Degrowth
Degrowth is a political, economic, and social movement that advocates for the intentional reduction of production and consumption to enhance ecological sustainability, social equity, and well-being, rather than pursuing perpetual economic growth.

It argues that the widespread assumption that GDP growth is the only way to sustain a good quality of living is false and has harmful social and ecological consequences. In this post I want to analyze the various arguments for Degrowth, some common criticism of the movement and what does this all means for us individually. I also share some thoughts for other environmentally conscious mathematicians on what is the role of mathematics in all of this. Not because I believe mathematics is the answer to this problem, quite the opposite, but it is the tool I have been given and I believe it will play a (and more) part in this.

Yes but, why?

The Degrowth movement can be argued for a variety of angles, from social to ecologic ones. In this paragraph we'll start by talking about the ecological argument for Degrowth. Well actually the argument is fairly straightforward: One way to pollute less, is to produce less, but since production is tightly linked with the GPD this means shrinking the economy. So the argument we'll actually analyze is more the answer to the questions "Is it actually necessary to stop increasing production?"

Unsustainable energy demand

The economy growth by about 3% every year. This may not seem like much but it still is an exponential growth and means that the economy will double every 23.45 years. Furthermore since energy is tightly correlated to GDP, this means that unless something changes quickly, our energy consumption will also more or less double in the same amount of time. Now even In the magical world where we are only using renewables, these do not come from nothing, they require materials and the supply of al this materials will quickly finish when the demand for them is exponential. We are already being too slow in transitioning with the current energy demand, let alone an exponentially growing one.

Decoupling

This reasoning has an assumption: That GDP growth will be forever linked with energy consumption. This is what a lot of occidental countries are trying to change (CIE). But various studies either think that this is not possible at all (((CITE))) or that it cannot happen fast enough (((CITE))). And this makes a lot of sense, as efficient as you can be, the GDP comes down to production of value and when you producing something energy is needed. Of course this is true for GDP-energy decoupling, but what we might say we are actually interested in is GDP-CO2emissions decoupling. The literature I cited seems to move in the same direction, this is not really happening at all, and if it will it will not happen fast enough. Further more, the idea of simply ignoring GDP-energy productions sweeps under the rag the material problem that comes with it. The raw materials that we would need to sustain current or growing levels of energy for renewable sources will have a great ecological and political impact we are currently not talking about enough. This is the same kind of not thinking about possible consequences that brought us in this mess in the first place. Simply substituting not renewable sources with renewable one. I'm of course not advocating for not using renewables, the contrary, but we should not repeat the mistakes of the past, we should become more mindful and scrupulous about the use of new technologies and how they interact with the world. A very common opinion both in the literature and in the real world is that even if such decoupling is not happening fast enough, that the market, pushed by the demand of cheap energy will produce a new technology to make it possible. I personally believe that this is a deeply problematic way to think of this, we can not base the future of humanity on hope on some sort of savior technology we have no evidence for, otherwise its just wishful thinking (DOES THIS MEAN SOMETHING ELSE?)

Conclusion

In conclusion, it appears we have no choice but to find a way for our society to continue functioning without relying on economic growth if we are to effectively address climate change.

Even if we choose to disregard climate change, ignoring its potentially catastrophic consequences, we are still faced with an imminent challenge: peak oil has already been reached, occurring 20 years ago. The era of abundant, almost free energy that significantly elevated living standards in the global north is nearing its end. This shift challenges the long-held assumption that we will always have sufficient energy for everything. However, it's important to end on a hopeful note. The Degrowth movement offers tangible proposals for sustaining society without relying on individual sacrifices. Contrary to prioritizing economic growth, which often comes at the cost of the environment and our quality of life—leaving us more isolated and with less free time—the Degrowth movement advocates for a shift in focus. Much like how moving beyond the third postulate of Euclidean geometry opened up new worlds of understanding, rethinking the necessity of perpetual economic growth could unveil alternative, viable societal models. This article aims to illustrate that such an alternative world is not only deeply appealing but also achievable.

How do we get there?

I would like to start on a bright note by saying that some Degrowth mindset is already present in various institution and in our culture. This is not a new idea we all need to be convinced of from scratch. All the regulations governing minimum industry requirements for employees standards of living such as minimum wage, holydays and others are in some sense "Degrowth". What I mean is that we are already not maximizing profit at the (total) expanse of everything else. We are still too much focused on growth of course, but not totally, and this is a big difference. There are already some degrowth ideas and values in our society. But in some ways a totally degrowth society would have to look much different from ours and many things are not obvious at all on how they could function. In this paragraph I would like to talk about how we could sustainably (from a quality of life sense) transition to a degrowth society.

Shortened work-week

Maximazing profit has lead to the practice of throws away unsold products in various sector (((CITE))). That is, we are producing for the sake of producing and not to meet the actual demand. Thus we are already in the situation where we could de working less, reducing the production of not needed goods. This is not obvious but this policy (or related) are actually foundamental to transition to a more sustainable society. More free time also means more time for community work. This makes space for a lot of more community based solutions for personal needs we would have more time to take care of our need and of those of others , depending on our skills and disposition, we would have more time to devote to communal gardens, repair cafes or help out with needs of our local community. Voluntary community work takes us out of our insulations and keeps the community united. Community based solutions are more resilient (((CITE))). As a side note, less working time would give us the opportunity to travel slower, we would not need to take the plane because we don't have time and taking a longer bus or train ride would also be ok.

I like to think of this the following way. Just as when we walt to be silent we need to walk slowly and carefully thin of each step, If we want to diminish our impact on the environment we need to live slower: work less and work on what the community actually need, grow our slow food, slowly (compared to rebuying) repair our things and take the time to travel.
